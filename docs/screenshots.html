Signal Chain

How data gets from REAPER to your display

Four lightweight components, all running on commodity hardware. No proprietary protocols, no cloud, no latency surprises.

🎛
STEP 01
JSFX Plugin
Lives on your REAPER master bus. Computes LUFS, RMS, True Peak, phase correlation, and 16-band FFT in real time. Exposes values to the Lua host.
meterbridge_spectrum.jsfx
📜
STEP 02
Lua Bridge
A REAPER Lua script polls audio metrics and transport state every 20–50 ms, serializes them to a compact JSON packet, and hands it to the relay via stdout.
meterbridge_reaper_bridge.lua
🐍
STEP 03
Python Relay
Receives JSON from Lua, converts to the compact binary UDP protocol, and blasts it to the ESP32 over WiFi or USB serial. Also routes transport commands in reverse.
meterbridge_relay.py
📺
STEP 04
ESP32 Display
Parses UDP packets at 20 Hz, updates meter state, and re-renders the entire LVGL UI at up to 500 FPS on the 7" 800×480 touch display.
ESP32-S3 · LVGL v8.3

FFT Spectrum Analyzer

Six spectrum themes. One tap to switch.

The 16-band spectrum analyzer screen shows frequency content from 31 Hz to 16 kHz. Each bar has a white peak-hold marker. All six color themes shown below — captured live from a real session.

FIRE
FIRE Theme
Red → orange → yellow gradient by amplitude. Deep sub-bass dominant at 31–63 Hz, natural high-frequency rolloff above 1 kHz. Peak: 31 Hz at −18.7 dB.
31Hz peak Bass heavy Live session
About FIRE

The Fire theme maps amplitude to color temperature — cool reds for the hottest signal tapering to white at low levels. It's optimized for dark studio environments where a warm glow is less fatiguing than cool blues.

This session was at −4.6 LUFS-I, well into commercial loudness territory. The 31 Hz sub-bass dominance you see here is typical of modern hip-hop/electronic production.

Bands: 31Hz · 44Hz · 63Hz · 88Hz · 125Hz · 177Hz · 250Hz · 354Hz
        500Hz · 707Hz · 1kHz · 1.4kHz · 2kHz · 4kHz · 8kHz · 16kHz
CLASSIC
CLASSIC Theme
Cyan-to-blue gradient inspired by traditional hardware spectrum analyzers. Cooler palette, easier on the eyes during long sessions. Midrange content at 250 Hz–1 kHz clearly visible.
Cyan → Blue Eye-friendly
About CLASSIC

The Classic theme uses a cyan-to-deep-blue gradient that resembles vintage hardware spectrum bridge units. It provides maximum readability under studio fluorescent lighting.

Notice the midrange resonance lift at 250 Hz–1 kHz — the wider spread here compared to Fire reveals a different musical moment in the session.

RAINBOW
RAINBOW Theme
Each frequency bin has its own color — red at sub-bass through violet at high frequencies. The most visually distinctive theme, excellent for frequency identification at a glance.
Sub-bass red HF blue Most vivid
About RAINBOW

Rainbow maps frequency position (not amplitude) to hue. Sub-bass sits in red, low-mids in orange/yellow, mids in green, highs in cyan-blue. This makes it trivially easy to identify which frequency ranges are active.

The teal spike visible around 1 kHz–1.4 kHz in this capture shows an interesting dip-and-rise in that frequency range — likely a room resonance or deliberate mix notch.

GHOST
GHOST Theme
Monochrome teal-green, no gradient coloring. Pure signal-to-shape visualization without color distraction. Preferred by engineers who want shape over spectacle.
Monochrome Clinical
About GHOST

Ghost removes all amplitude-based coloring. The monochrome presentation forces you to read the shape of the spectrum rather than being drawn to color hotspots. Ideal for mastering engineers who want neutral reference.

In this capture you can see the smooth natural rolloff of the high frequencies above 4 kHz — cleaner than the Fire capture, suggesting this was taken at a quieter passage.

OCEAN
OCEAN Theme
Deep navy-blue to teal gradient. Subdued and elegant — the "dark mode" of the spectrum themes. Peak at 31 Hz near −10 dB shows a louder passage than the other captures.
Navy → Teal Subdued
About OCEAN

Ocean uses a navy-to-teal gradient that integrates naturally into dark room environments. It's lower in perceived brightness than Fire or Rainbow, reducing eye strain during extended monitoring sessions. The deep blue at the base and teal at the peaks create a wave-like appearance.

First Live Capture — Fire Theme
The very first spectrum screenshot captured at the moment the user navigated to the spectrum screen live during a REAPER session. Peak at 177 Hz, −13.3 dB. White peak-hold markers visible across all 16 bands.
177Hz peak −13.3 dB Authentic
About Peak Hold Markers

The white horizontal dash above each bar is the peak-hold marker. It stays at the highest value seen for a configurable hold time (default 0.5s "Snappy" — also available in 2s, 5s, or ∞ forever).

The spectrum is computed by the JSFX plugin using a 256–2048 point FFT, downmixed to 16 frequency bands covering the perceptually important range from 31 Hz to 16 kHz.

Settings & Configuration

Everything configurable from the display

No config files, no command line. Every setting is accessible from the touchscreen Settings page and persists across reboots in NVS flash.

⚙️ Settings Page — All Options
  • WiFi network scan & connection with password entry
  • Connection mode: WiFi UDP · USB Serial · Both
  • Tick rate: 20/s (50ms) up to 1/s slow mode
  • Meter source: MASTER bus · SELECTED track
  • Display rotation: 0° · 90° · 180° · 270°
  • Peak hold time: Snappy · 2s · 5s · Forever
  • Meter height: Normal · Large · XL · XXL Giant
  • Loudness target: −9 to −23 LUFS presets
  • 11 meter themes + 6 spectrum themes
